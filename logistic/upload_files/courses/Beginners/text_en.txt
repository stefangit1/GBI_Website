<h2 id ="was_trading">3.0 What is Trading and What is Forex?</h2>
	<h3 id ="begriff_trading">3.1 Clarifying the Term Trading</h3>
			<p>You have now reached the point where your account is set up with your broker, and a suitable trading program is installed. But what exactly are we doing, what are we trading, how does it all work, and what is the difference from investing?
			<br>
			Trading means "trade" in English, and it is nothing more than that. We buy something, trying to sell it later at a higher or lower price to make a profit. Yes, a trader can also make money when prices fall, but more on that later.
			</p>
		
		<h4 id ="beispiel_tauschhandel">3.1.1 Example of Simple Barter</h4>
			<p>For better understanding, we have prepared an example.
			We are at a large marketplace that deals in gold. We don't own any gold yet, so we want to buy a bar. We have €100 in our wallet. The current price of the bar is €100, so we go to one of the sellers and buy a bar.
			</p>
			<img src="../logistic/upload_files/courses/Beginners/handelbsp1.jpg" alt="Simple Trade">
			<p>Now we own a gold bar that is currently worth €100. Two days later, we return to the market with our gold bar, but the supply of gold is tiny, and the demand is high, as suddenly everyone wants to own gold. As a result, the price of gold has risen by €30 over the two days, and we can sell it again.</p>
			<img src="../logistic/upload_files/courses/Beginners/handelbsp2.jpg" alt="Simple Trade">
			<p>In this example, we would have made a €30 profit, and our trade would have been successful. Now we know that we can buy something cheaply and sell it at a higher price, but how does a price actually form?</p>
		
		<h4 id="beispiel_kursbildung">3.1.2 Example of Simple Price Formation</h4>	
			<p>In your trading career, you will hear the terms "supply" and "demand" more often than any other financial terms, and not without reason, because these two conditions are the reason why the price of a security, currency pair, or, as in our example, the value of gold, moves at all. And please correct me if I'm wrong, but there is no secret formula for how a price magically forms. It just so happens that when supply exceeds demand, the seller must lower the price, causing the price to fall, and when demand exceeds supply, the seller can drive the price up, causing the price to rise.
			<br> But let's apply this again to our previous example to better illustrate the situation.
			<br><br>We are back at our marketplace full of traders. We have €130 in our pocket, and we want to buy another bar to sell it later when the price rises. However, today, the newspaper reported that gold is the future, and therefore thousands of businessmen urgently want to buy gold at this marketplace today. Demand is high, but unfortunately, supply is low because only a few are selling gold at this marketplace today. The traders know exactly that they can make a profit today. They slowly feel their way up. The first stall sells for €132, the second for €137, and the third for €150. The first and second also raise their prices to €150, and people are still buying. Conclusion: The price of gold rises.</p>
			<img src="../logistic/upload_files/courses/Beginners/handelbsp3.jpg" alt="Marketplace">
			<p>The next day, we watch the news. The anchor says that owning gold is officially going to be banned. Naturally, the crowd from the previous day will rush to the marketplace and try to sell the gold they bought yesterday, but we have a problem—no one wants to buy it anymore! Even the traders want to get rid of it. What do you do when you want to get rid of something, but no one wants it? Exactly, you lower the price. On our marketplace, the price is no longer being raised slowly; instead, it is being rapidly lowered in the hope that someone will buy it for a low price. The price falls. A struggle ensues because the person who offers the lowest price will most likely get rid of the gold the quickest.</p>
			<img src="../logistic/upload_files/courses/Beginners/handelbsp4.jpg" alt="Marketplace">
			<p>While we have used an extreme example here, this method allows us to show you how a price is formed. Later, we will discuss topics like liquidity, volatility, and other terms, but for now, it's too early for that.
			<br>We now know what trading is and what lies behind the magical formation of prices. Now the only question left is, "What is Forex?"</p>


	<h3 id ="was_forex">3.2 What is Forex?</h3>
		<p>Now that we know what trading is—trading—and roughly understand how a price is formed, we can move on to the actual trading object. This is Foreign Exchange, also known as Forex or, in English, currency trading. When we talk about currency trading, we mean trading between two currencies, such as exchanging Euros for US Dollars [EUR/USD]. Together, the two currencies form a pair, called a currency pair. I think many people may not realize that they have already engaged in Forex trading at some point. Think of an exchange office during a vacation, where you had to exchange Euros for a foreign currency, such as Lira, Dollars, Kuna [Croatia before the Euro], etc., at a certain exchange rate. In principle, this exchange is already Forex, only here we are doing it to make money, not to spend it. We exchange our currency for another with the goal that the exchange rate of our currency will rise.
		<br>If we refer back to our previous example, we just need to replace the gold with another currency, and we have a good example for illustration.</p>
		
		<h4 id="long_pos">3.2.1 Long Position</h4>
		<p style="color: red;">IMPORTANT! TERM LONG</p>
    		<p>If we have the symbol <strong>[EUR/USD]</strong> and exchange it, we are buying Euros with USD. This is called a <strong>Long Position</strong>. In other words, we go long and hope that the exchange rate rises.</p>
    		<p>You can also bet on falling prices, but that will be discussed later.</p>
		<img src="../logistic/upload_files/courses/Beginners/handelbsp5.jpg" alt="Trading">
		<p>We have $100 in our pocket. The exchange rate between EUR/USD is 1.08114. This means that if we buy Euros for $100, we get €92.49.
		<br>We now have our €92.49, which, when exchanged, would be $100. But now the exchange rate of the currency pair rises to 1.10000. If we now exchange back from EUR to USD [1.1 * €92.49 = $101.739], we have made a profit of $1.739. Of course, the profit is small, but if you keep reading, you will later learn about leverage, which can generate a significant amount of money from small movements, but more on that later.</p>		

		<h4 id="short_pos">3.2.2 Short Position</h4>
		<p style="color: red;">IMPORTANT! TERM SHORT</p>
		<p>But how do we make money when prices fall? Imagine we virtually borrow €100. At some point, we have to pay the money back. But before that happens, we exchange the €100 at an exchange rate of 1.8 for $108. If the exchange rate now drops from 1.8 to 1.6, we can repay the €100 we borrowed more cheaply and keep the rest as profit. So now the exchange rate is 1.6, and we only need $106 to exchange it for €100 and pay off our debt. This leaves us with a profit of $2.</p>

<h2 id="begriffe_eigenschaften">4.0 Terms and Characteristics of Forex</h2>

<h3 id="hebelprodukt">4.1 Leverage Product</h3>
<p>Forex is a leveraged product. But what does that mean? It is a way to make a lot of money with little money, or to lose it quickly. A leveraged product works as follows: you have a deposit and leverage. Simply put, if I deposit $500 into my account and have leverage of 100, I can trade with <strong>[500$ * 100 = 50,000$]</strong>.</p>
<p>This does not mean that I own $50,000 or that I can lose that much. No, I just have the ability to trade with that capital. But as soon as my $500 is gone, most brokers will close the position. More on this later in the margin section. So if I keep losing money and drop to $49,500 in our example, my money is gone. Either the broker forbids further trading and blocks transactions, or it allows you to incur debt, which must then be repaid.</p>
<ul>
    <li>I deposit $100 into my account | Leverage = 100 | Total amount I can manage: $10,000</li>
    <li>Deposit $1000 | Leverage = 10 | Total amount I can manage: $10,000</li>
</ul>
<img src="../logistic/upload_files/courses/Beginners/hebel1.jpg" alt="Leverage">
<p>Leverage typically ranges from 5 to 500, but it should be noted that leverage up to 100 is usually available for regular users, and only professional traders are allowed to trade with such high leverage. Nevertheless, even with leverage of 50 or 100, it's possible to move a lot of money with little capital, resulting in either losses or gains from small price movements due to the large positions involved. So, with $100 and a leverage of 100, we can exchange $10,000 at a rate of 1.08 for 9,259€. If the rate now rises from 1.08 to 1.09 and we exchange our euros back into dollars, we get $10,092 and have thus made a profit of $92. In conclusion, we made $92 with our $100 due to a small price increase, nearly doubling our money. It is important to note that if the rate had gone from 1.08 to 1.07, we would have lost $92 and would have only $8 left.</p>

<h3 id="lot">4.2 What is a Lot?</h3>
<p>When we talk about a lot, we refer to the size of the position. We distinguish between standardized sizes:</p>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Standard Lot:</strong> 100,000 units</li>
    <li><strong>Mini Lot:</strong> 10,000 units (0.1 of a standard lot)</li>
    <li><strong>Micro Lot:</strong> 1,000 units (0.01 of a standard lot)</li>
    <li><strong>Nano Lot:</strong> 100 units (0.001 of a standard lot)</li>
</ul>
<p>It is important to note that the nano size is almost never used and is therefore not even available in MetaTrader5.</p>

<p><br>Now we are trading <strong>EUR/USD</strong> and going <strong>long</strong>. Our deposit is $1000 and we have leverage of 100, which corresponds to $100,000.</p>
<p><br>If we exchange a standard lot into euros, we exchange our entire $100,000 into euros. But if we only exchange 0.1 of a standard lot, i.e., a mini lot, we only exchange $10,000 into euros.</p>
<img src="../logistic/upload_files/courses/Beginners/auftragsfenster1.jpg" alt="Order Window">
<p>This figure you have just seen is the window in MetaTrader5 where positions are purchased, i.e., where you execute a currency exchange. In MetaTrader5, the volume refers to lots. This is indicated here in a standard lot, as mentioned in our previous example. So, if we want to exchange 1,000 units of our currency, we enter 0.01.</p>

What exactly does this mean, and what do we mean by session? A session is the period during which a specific region or, more precisely, a certain city starts trading. During the session of the city, the exchange opens, and the most liquidity is present, depending on the significance of the session. This may seem like information that not everyone understands right away, so we naturally dedicate an example to this topic.</p>

This is because both stock speculators and local traders and banks are active during trading hours. When a session starts, it becomes day, and banks, exchange offices, and trading are open. At night, people sleep, and the exchanges are closed.</p>

<h3 id="wahrungspaare">4.5 What Currency Pairs Are There?</h3>
<p>The different pairs are divided into three major categories:</p>
<ul>
    <li>Majors</li>
    <li>Minors</li>
    <li>Exotics</li>

  <h4 id="spread">4.5.1 What is the Spread?</h4>
<p>Before we explain what makes these groups special, we need to define the term <strong>Spread</strong>. In trading, there are always two prices: the Ask price and the Bid price. The Ask price is the price at which the market is willing to exchange. This means that if I want to buy USD in exchange for EUR, I do not buy at the current rate, but rather at a slightly higher price, the price at which the seller is willing to exchange. Conversely, if I want to convert my EUR back into USD, I receive the Bid price, which indicates the price at which the buyer is willing to buy. The difference between the Bid and Ask prices is the Spread, which is measured in pips.</p>

<h4 id="majors">4.5.2 Majors</h4>
<p>The Majors are global major currency pairs that are typically traded the most and therefore have a very low spread due to high liquidity. These pairs consist of two major currencies, such as EUR or USD. Currently, there are 7 Majors, which are as follows:</p>
<ul>
    <li>EUR/USD - Euro/US Dollar</li>
    <li>USD/JPY - US Dollar/Japanese Yen</li>
    <li>GBP/USD - British Pound/US Dollar</li>
    <li>AUD/USD - Australian Dollar/US Dollar</li>
    <li>USD/CHF - US Dollar/Swiss Franc</li>
    <li>USD/CAD - US Dollar/Canadian Dollar</li>
    <li>NZD/USD - New Zealand Dollar/US Dollar</li>
</ul>
<p>We see that in each of these pairs, the USD is involved. We recommend trading these pairs due to their low spread and the ease of exchange.</p>

<h4 id="minors">4.5.3 Minors</h4>
<p>The Minors are also known as Cross Pairs. These pairs consist of two currencies, neither of which includes the USD. In other words, these are currencies from the Major category (excluding USD) that are traded among themselves.</p>
<ul>
    <li>EUR/GBP - Euro/British Pound</li>
    <li>EUR/AUD - Euro/Australian Dollar</li>
    <li>GBP/JPY - British Pound/Japanese Yen</li>
    <li>AUD/JPY - Australian Dollar/Japanese Yen</li>
    <li>EUR/CAD - Euro/Canadian Dollar</li>
    <li>NZD/JPY - New Zealand Dollar/Japanese Yen</li>
    <li>GBP/AUD - British Pound/Australian Dollar</li>
</ul>
<p>These are not traded as frequently as the Majors, but it is still possible to trade these pairs efficiently. The spread is higher, but usually only about double that of the Majors. For example, the spread for Majors is mostly between 1 and 3 pips, while for Minors it ranges from 3 to 7 pips.</p>

<h4 id="exotics">4.5.4 Exotics</h4>
<p>The Exotics include one major currency and one currency from a country that is not frequently traded. The name <strong>Exotics</strong> indicates that this trading is relatively illiquid (illiquid is the opposite of liquid). These pairs are traded very rarely and thus have high spreads, which can range from 10 to 100 pips.</p>
<ul>
    <li>USD/TRY - US Dollar/Turkish Lira</li>
    <li>EUR/TRY - Euro/Turkish Lira</li>
    <li>USD/SGD - US Dollar/Singapore Dollar</li>
    <li>EUR/THB - Euro/Thai Baht</li>
    <li>USD/ZAR - US Dollar/South African Rand</li>
    <li>USD/HKD - US Dollar/Hong Kong Dollar</li>
    <li>USD/PLN - US Dollar/Polish Zloty</li>
</ul>
<p>If you look at the right side of the pair, you will notice that these currencies are important in their respective countries but have little significance in the global market. This leads to a certain lack of liquidity in trading these pairs, making the exchange very costly. Therefore, we recommend avoiding Exotics, especially as a beginner, and focusing first on the Majors.</p>

<h3 id="margin">4.6 What is Margin?</h3>
<p>Margin is the minimum requirement needed to enter a trade. In principle, this topic is very important, but as long as you don't handle exaggerated positions and don't stretch your account to the last cent, it doesn't play a major role at the moment. If you are interested, you can simply search Google for a suitable explanation and read it in detail now or later after this course. We will briefly move to practice to prepare you for trading. Go to MetaTrader5 and navigate to "Trade" at the bottom. There, under "Free Margin," you can see how much of your capital you can still use.</p>
